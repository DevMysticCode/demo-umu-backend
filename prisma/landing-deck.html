The market today
Why we built it
UK home sales
are broken.
A third of all sales collapse before they reach completion. Of the ones that do, the average takes nearly six months — almost all of it spent waiting for paperwork that could have been ready on day one. Here's what's actually going wrong, what's being done about it, and why we built UMU.
The numbers
179days → 14days
12× slower
UK avg vs. with Passport
The average UK home sale takes 179 days from offer accepted to completion. Almost all of that time is the buyer's solicitor asking for documents the seller didn't have ready — title deeds, planning permissions, FENSA certificates, surveys, fittings forms. With a Property Passport in place from day one, that drops to around 14 days.
1 in 3
Sales collapse
No safety net before completion
Roughly 33% of agreed UK property sales fall through before completion. The most common cause is a problem revealed in the buyer's solicitor's enquiries weeks into the transaction — something the seller didn't know about, or didn't think mattered. By that point both sides have spent thousands.
£2,700
Lost per consumer
Per failed sale, on average
The average buyer or seller burns about £2,700 per fall-through — survey fees, legal fees, mortgage application fees, removal deposits, and the personal cost of stress and time off work. Multiply across the country and the figure is roughly £900 million a year in waste.
Why it happens
The English property system has a quiet design flaw at its heart: everything important is revealed at the wrong time. A buyer falls in love with a house, makes an offer, gets it accepted — and only then do the questions begin. Where are the title deeds? Has the loft conversion got building regs? Is there a covenant on the boundary? When was the boiler last serviced?
The seller scrambles. Most of these answers exist somewhere — in a drawer, an old email, a council archive — but finding them takes weeks. While the seller hunts, the buyer waits. Their mortgage offer expires. Their patience runs out. Or worse, a survey reveals damp, or a search flags a planning issue, or a leasehold condition turns out to be more onerous than it looked. The buyer pulls out. The seller starts again.
The system assumes discovery should happen during the transaction. That's the design flaw. By the time problems surface, they're expensive, emotional, and binary — fix it now or lose the sale. Most sellers had no idea anything was wrong. They just wanted to move.
"You don't know what you don't know — until you're three weeks in and the buyer's solicitor asks for something you can't find."
Industry observation Illustrative
What's changing
Policy update
The government is acting.
The case for upfront information has been understood at a policy level for some time. Mandation is coming — every UK property will be required to have a Property Passport-equivalent record before being put on the market. The aim is to surface what buyers' solicitors will inevitably ask for, before the offer stage rather than after it. Estate agents, conveyancers, and consumer bodies have been broadly supportive. UMU has been engaged in the conversations leading up to this change, and is one of the providers preparing for the new regime.

01 Before
Why she started early.
Aisha had watched her parents lose a sale four years ago. The buyer's solicitor asked for FENSA certificates her mother couldn't find, and after two months of searching, the buyer pulled out. They lost £3,400 in survey fees. Her mother started again from scratch.
When Aisha decided to sell her two-bed semi in Coventry, she didn't want to risk the same outcome. She started preparing the paperwork three weeks before her first viewing.
"I didn't want my house to fall through because the buyer's solicitor needed a bit of paper I couldn't find."
— Aisha
02 During
A passport, ready before the first viewing.
Aisha set up her Property Passport in late February. Title deeds came through automatically from HM Land Registry. The Coventry council's planning portal returned her search results in three days. The 2019 FENSA certificate — the one her mother couldn't find — was already on file with the installer.
By the time the agent put a board up, the Passport was complete. Fourteen sections, all verified, all ready to share. She listed on March 3rd.
03 After
Fourteen days, start to finish.
Two viewings on the first weekend. An offer at the asking price by Wednesday. The buyer's solicitor downloaded Aisha's Passport on Thursday morning — and by Thursday afternoon, they'd raised exactly two enquiries, both about the loft conversion.
Both questions had answers waiting in the Passport. Building regs sign-off from 2014. The original architect's plan. Searches were already in. Exchange happened on day 11. Completion on day 14.
"We had a Passport ready before our first viewing. The buyer's solicitor had everything they needed in a day. No surveys lost, no fall-through."
— Aisha
